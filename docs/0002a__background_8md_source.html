1 Background {#background}
2 =========================
3 
4 A note regarding nomenclature: Healy (2010) gives
5 clear definitions for a number of recharge-related terms:
6 
7 - potential recharge: water that has infiltrated into the root zone;
8  this water may leave the bottom of the root zone, eventually
9  becoming recharge, or it may be removed from the soil column by
10  means of evaporation and transpiration.
11 - deep percolation or net infiltration: water that has escaped the
12  root zone and will eventually find its way to the groundwater table.
13 
14 In this and the previous documentation of SWB, potential recharge is
15 defined as water that leaves the bottom of the root zone and may
16 become
17 groundwater recharge after transiting the remainder of the unsaturated
18 zone. The reason for using the term potential recharge in this manner
19 was to emphasize the point that SWB does not simulate any processes
20 beneath the root zone, and reflects the fact that there may be a
21 considerable delay between the time the water leaves the bottom of the
22 root zone and the time it meets the groundwater table. The term
23 potential recharge as used in this document is the same as Healy's
24 (2010) deep percolation or net infiltration.
25 
26 Groundwater recharge can vary greatly over time and space.
27 Site-specific
28 data, when available, are not applicable to regional-scale problems.
29 Groundwater modelers often assume that a fraction of precipitation is
30 converted to recharge, or they instead use recharge as a calibration
31 parameter. In transient groundwater-modeling problems, use of a
32 physically based, spatially variable recharge boundary condition has
33 been found to improve model performance (Jyrkama and Sykes, 2007).
34 
35 Numerical modeling is one technique sometimes used to supply a
36 spatially
37 varied, transient recharge boundary condition on a regional scale
38 (Scanlon and others, 2002). Simple soil-water-balance models are a
39 category of numerical models commonly applied to groundwater recharge
40 estimation problems. There perhaps are hundreds of soil-water-balance
41 models described in the literature. Many soil-water-balance models
42 were
43 developed in order to evaluate crop irrigation requirements and
44 impacts
45 (Kendy and others, 2003), crop yield prediction
46 (Akinremi and others, 1996), and landfill cover design
47 (Schroeder and others, 1994).
48 
49 Similarly, there are many examples of groundwater recharge estimation
50 by
51 means of a soil-water-balance. For example, the U.S. Environmental
52 Protection Agency (U.S. EPA) HELP model, a soil-water-balance code
53 used
54 in landfill design (Schroeder and others, 1994), has been linked to
55 commercial geographic information system (GIS) software
56 (Jyrkama and Sykes, 2007). WetSpass calculates long-term recharge by
57 means
58 of a soil-water-balance model within a commercial GIS software package
59 (Batelaan and De Smedt, 2001). (Finch, 2001) describes a
60 distributed daily soil-water-balance model, but does not specify the
61 computing platform.
62 
63 The SWB code described in this report was derived from work completed
64 as
65 part of a doctoral dissertation at the University of Wisconsin-Madison
66 (Dripps, 2003; Dripps and Bradbury, 2007). This code was written in
67 Visual Basic for Applications inside of a Microsoft Excel spreadsheet
68 (Dripps, 2003).
69 
70 Each of the examples listed above either requires proprietary
71 software,
72 is implemented in a proprietary language, is complex to set up and
73 use,
74 or is not distributed in the public domain. For these reasons, in
75 2006,
76 the U.S. Geological Survey (USGS) translated the original
77 soil-water-balance code from Visual Basic to modern Fortran 95.
78 
79 Within the USGS, water balance models have long been used as a means
80 to
81 estimate potential groundwater recharge. The Yucca Mountain Project of
82 the 1980s and 1990s produced the INFIL 3.0 model
83 (U.S. Geological Survey, 2008). The Basin Characterization
84 Model (BCM) has been applied to significant tracts of the southwestern
85 United States (Flint and Flint, 2007; Flint and Flint, 2007). A
86 similar model was developed and applied in Montana, Idaho, and
87 Washington State (Bauer and Vaccaro, 1987; Bauer and Vaccaro, 1990). A
88 custom water balance model has been applied to the Hawaiian Islands
89 for
90 decades (Izuka and others, 2010, Engott (2011)). Another custom
91 water balance model was applied to the central Midwest regional
92 aquifer
93 system, with special emphasis on estimating consumptive use of water
94 and
95 the resulting impact on recharge (Dugan and Peckenpaugh, 1985). These
96 models
97 have generally been developed with specific environmental settings in
98 mind (Yucca Mountain, Hawaiian Islands), are known well to only their
99 authors, or would be difficult to apply to other more general use
100 cases.
101 However, these previous works also represent a significant body of
102 work
103 aimed at generating improved recharge estimates, and as time permits
104 the
105 good ideas and process implementations in those codes will be adapted
106 to
107 the SWB code.
108 
109 For reasons of ease of use, non-reliance on proprietary software, and
110 in
111 the interest of general applicability, the U.S. Geological Survey
112 (USGS)
113 translated the original soil-water-balance code from Visual Basic to
114 modern Fortran 95 (Westenbroek and others, 2010). This code was
115 derived
116 from work completed as part of a doctoral dissertation at the
117 University
118 of Wisconsin-Madison (Dripps, 2003). The SWB code has evolved
119 since the original release, with crop water demand calculations and
120 addition of some of the functionality of the Hawaiian Water Budget
121 Code
122 (Izuka and others, 2010).
