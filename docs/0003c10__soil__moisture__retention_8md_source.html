1 Soil-Moisture Retention / Actual Evapotranspiration {#soil_moisture_retention}
2 =================================================================================
3 
4 [TOC]
5 
6 In the days following a rainstorm, soil moisture is close to field
7 capacity, and moisture is evaporated from bare soil and transpired by
8 plants at rates close to the maximum rate sustainable given climatic
9 conditions. Assuming no further precipitation, in subsequent days the
10 evaporation and transpiration rates decrease as remaining
11 soil-moisture
12 is held more tightly within the soil matrix (Dunne and Leopold, 1978).
13 
14 One simple way of simulating decreasing rates of soil-moisture
15 evapotranspiration is to assume that the actual evapotranspiration is
16 some function of the potential or reference evapotranspiration and the
17 current soil-moisture amount eq. 1.
18 
19 $$AET = PET \\cdot f\\left( {\\frac{\\theta }{{{\\theta _{fc}}}}}
20 \\right)\\qquad(1)$$
21 
22 where $AET$ is the actual evapotranspiration, $PET$ is the potential
23 evapotranspiration, $\\theta$ is the current soil-moisture amount
24 [L],
25 and $\\theta_{fc}$ is the soil field-capacity.
26 
27 This section discusses the two soil-moisture retention functions
28 implemented in SWB, one developed by Thornthwaite
29 (1948) and the other included in the FAO-56
30 approach (1998).
31 
32 Thornthwaite-Mather {#sm_thornthwaite_mather}
33 ================================================
34 
35 The first versions of SWB included full tabularized versions of the
36 soil-moisture retention function, along with methods to interpolate
37 between the various table values. The original published
38 Thornthwaite-Mather (1957) method also
39 introduces a variable (accumulated potential water loss, APWL) to
40 track
41 the cumulative unmet potential evapotranspiration as a way to make
42 calculation simpler. Ultimately, the many steps involved in the
43 published method are designed to estimate actual evapotranspiration as
44 directly proportional to the soil moisture relative to field capacity

52 Daily soil moisture may be estimated from this relation by first
53 defining the instantaneous soil evapotranspiration as equal to the
54 change in storage:
55 
56 $$et_a = - \\frac{{d\\theta }}{{dt}}\\qquad(2)$$
57 
58 where $et_a$ is the instantaneous actual evapotranspiration, and
59 $\\frac{{d\\theta }}{{dt}}$ is the rate of change in soil moisture
60 relative to time.
61 
62 The relation shown in +fig. **??** can be used to define a function
63 relating actual and potential evapotranspiration as:
64 
65 $$et_a = et_p \\cdot \\frac{\\theta }{{{\\theta _{fc}}}}$$
66 
67 where $et_a$ is the instantaneous actual evapotranspiration, $et_p$
68 is
69 the instantaneous potential evapotranspiration, $\\theta$ is the soil
70 moisture, and $\\theta_{fc}$ is the soil moisture value at field
71 capacity.
72 
73 $$ - \\frac{{d\\theta }}{{dt}} = et_p \\cdot \\frac{\\theta }{{{\\theta
74 _{fc}}}}$$
75 
76 $$ - \\frac{{d\\theta }}{\\theta } = \\frac{{et_p}}{{{\\theta
77 _{fc}}}}dt$$
78 
79 $$ - \\int{\\frac{{d\\theta }}{\\theta }} = \\frac{1}{{{\\theta
80 _{fc}}}}\\int {et_p dt} $$
81 
82 $$ \\left. { - \\ln \\theta } \\right|*{{\\theta *{t - 1}}}^{{\\theta
83 *t}} = \\frac{1}{{{\\theta *{fc}}}}E{T_p}$$
84 
85 $$ {\\theta *t} = {\\theta *{t - 1}} \\cdot {e^{\\left( { -
86 \\frac{{E{T_p}}}{{{\\theta _{fc}}}}} \\right)}}$$

88 FAO-56 {#sm_fao_56}
89 ======================
90 
91 The FAO-56 framework for evaluating plant water requirements contains
92 a
93 variation on the Thornthwaite soil-moisture retention scheme. In the
94 FAO-56 calculations, soil-moisture is assumed to be completely
95 available
96 to evaporation and transpiration until it reaches some critical
97 soil-moisture level ($\\theta_{crit}$). Thereafter, the relation
98 between
99 actual ET and potential ET is linear with slope
100 $\\frac{1}{\\theta_{crit}}$ +Fig. **??**.
