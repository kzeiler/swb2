6 Bucket {#bucket}
7 =================
8 
9 The "bucket" model of interception is the original interception
10 process
11 module that was coded into SWB. The bucket model simply assumes that a
12 set and constant amount of rainfall or snowfall must fall before the
13 soil will receive any precipitation.

15 Gash {#gash}
16 =============
17 
18 Another option in SWB for calculating canopy interception is a
19 modified
20 version of the model described by Gash and others
21 (1979), herein referred to as the Gash model. Using
22 this approach, canopy evaporation for a given day and location depends
23 on precipitation, forest structure, and the mean rates of evaporation
24 and precipitation. The Gash model was modified so that (1)
25 precipitation
26 includes rainfall and fog interception, instead of rain only, and (2)
27 water cannot be stored on the forest canopy for more than a day. The
28 forest structure is characterized in terms of canopy cover, canopy
29 capacity, trunk-storage capacity, and the proportion of precipitation
30 diverted to stemflow. Canopy cover, $c$ , is the fraction of a
31 forested
32 area that is covered by leaves, stems, and branches of trees. Canopy
33 capacity, $S$, is the depth of water left on the canopy when rainfall
34 and throughfall have ceased (Gash and Morton, 1978). Evaporation of
35 water from tree trunks is accounted for using the proportion of
36 precipitation that is diverted to stemflow, $p$, and trunk-storage
37 capacity, $k$, which is considered in terms of an equivalent depth of
38 precipitation. The last parameter needed for the Gash model is the
39 ratio
40 of the mean evaporation rate to the mean precipitation rate during
41 saturated conditions, $V$.
42 
43 To calculate canopy interception, the first step is to determine the
44 minimum depth of precipitation necessary to saturate the forest
45 canopy,
46 $P_{sat}$, which is calculated on the basis of equation 2 in Gash and
47 others (1995) as:
48 
49 $$P_{sat} = - \\frac{S}{{c \\cdot V}}\\ln \\left( {1 - V}
50 \\right)\\qquad(1)$$
51 
52 where
53 
54 $P_{sat}$ = precipitation necessary to saturate the canopy [L],
55 
56 $S$ = canopy capacity per unit of ground area [L] (a constant),
57 
58 $c$ = canopy cover per unit of ground area [dimensionless], and
59 
60 $V$ = ratio of mean evaporation rate to mean precipitation rate during
61 saturated conditions [dimensionless].
62 
63 On the basis of the revised analytical form of the Gash model
64 presented
65 in table 1 of Gash and others (1995), canopy interception for a given
66 day, (CE)i, is calculated for three canopy conditions as follows:
67 
68 for $P_i &lt; P_{sat}$,
69 
70 $(CE)_i = c \\cdot P_i$ ,
71 
72 for ${P_i} \\geqslant {P_{sat}}$ and ${P_i} \\leqslant \\frac{k}{p}$,
73 
74 $(CE)*i = c \\cdot {P*{sat}}+ c \\cdot V \\cdot (P_i – P_{sat}) + p
75 \\cdot P_i$,
76 
77 for ${P_i} \\geqslant {P_{sat}}$ and ${P_i} &gt; \\frac{k}{p}$,
78 
79 $(CE)*i = c \\cdot P*{sat} + c \\cdot V \\cdot ({P_i} – P_{sat}) + k$,
80 
81 where:
82 
83 $k$ = trunk-storage capacity [L] (a constant), and
84 
85 $p$ = proportion of precipitation diverted to stemflow
86 [dimensionless].
87 
88 Advantages of the Gash model are: (1) it accounts for gaps in the
89 forest
90 canopy, which allows for a sparse canopy to be differentiated from a
91 dense canopy; (2) canopy interception during a period of precipitation
92 is dependent on the amount of precipitation during that period; and
93 (3)
94 the Gash model can account for spatial differences in climate.
95 Disadvantages of the Gash model are that it is theoretical and may be
96 difficult to adequately parameterize.

98 Horton {#horton}
99 =================
100 
101 Robert Horton made countless observations at his hydrologic laboratory
102 in the early 1900's. One of the hydrologic processes to receive his
103 attention is that of canopy interception. The Horton model begins with
104 a
105 "bucket" that must be filled regardless of total storm volume, and
106 adds
107 a linear relation that produces an increasing canopy interception
108 value
109 proportional to increasing storm volume (Horton, 1919).
